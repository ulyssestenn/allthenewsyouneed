## Note

Selection window: roughly 1:35 p.m. UTC July 13 through 1:35 p.m. UTC July 14, 2026. All 20 links were pulled directly from each outlet's own live feed, or (for AP) cross-checked against the outlet's own homepage markup, not fabricated. WSJ was fetched via its public Dow Jones RSS feeds (World News, US Business, US News, Politics, Economy, Tech, Health, Arts) using a browser-style User-Agent; two of the five WSJ picks (the CPI report and the Warsh testimony) come from a feed whose "title" field is a full descriptive sentence rather than a short headline, so a conventional headline was written from that sentence and the article's own URL slug — the link itself is the exact one published in WSJ's feed. Direct `curl` fetches of WSJ article pages returned 401 (subscriber paywall), which is normal for article pages as opposed to feeds. NYT was fetched via its public Top Stories RSS feed with no access issues. NBC's general feed (`feeds.nbcnews.com/nbcnews/public/news`) was dominated by World Cup semifinal video clips, Telemundo Spanish-language sports segments, and shopping content; the headlines above are the real hard-news items that were present in that same feed. Google News's site-restricted search (`site:apnews.com when:1d`) was used to identify AP's most current stories as instructed, but its RSS links are obfuscated `news.google.com` redirects that don't resolve to `apnews.com` URLs without a browser; the direct `apnews.com` URLs above were instead located by fetching AP's own server-rendered homepage and matching its embedded story list against the same headlines/topics surfaced by the Google News search, so both sources agree on which stories are current. Selection prioritized the U.S.-Iran conflict over the Strait of Hormuz (military escalation and its oil-market fallout), the June CPI report and Fed Chair Kevin Warsh's first congressional testimony, and the pattern of deaths in ICE immigration enforcement operations (the Maine shooting) as the throughlines that recurred independently across multiple outlets, alongside outlet-specific significant stories (a 12-state antitrust suit against the Paramount-Warner merger, New York's data-center moratorium, a rare Supreme Court funding request to Congress, a Chinese Politburo purge, a judge's rebuke of a Trump administration IRS lawsuit, and subpoenas to NYT reporters described as a press-freedom threat), while excluding World Cup coverage, Bastille Day pageantry, celebrity deaths and weddings (Sam Neill, Taylor Swift), and other novelty- or virality-driven items (a loose capybara in England, a raccoon break-in).
